# Common Magic: Folklore and the Aliveness of Things

*The pages of this book are rough and smell of salt, iron, and old earth. They seem to hum with a low, vibrating frequency. These are the truths that even the professors handle with caution.*

### The Magic of the Ordinary
The Labyrinth recognizes that the "Climax" (the Unwritten Chapter) is built of ancient, simple foundations. The magic here is not about wands or incantations; it's about the **Choice of Perception**.

**The Salt Circle:**
Salt isn't just a seasoning; it is the **Inhibitor of the Formless**. It represents the *boundary*. When an Anchor in the Climax spills salt and throws it over their left shoulder, they are performing a primitive, but effective, "Warding Enchantment" against the Nothing’s encroachment.

**The Iron Rule:**
Iron is the **Density of the Story**. It is the thing that the Nothing cannot erase because iron *knows* what it is. To hold a piece of cold iron in the Climax is to anchor one's physical presence against the "flicker" of apathy.

### The Lunar Turn (Common Rhythms)
The Moon is the **Labyrinth’s Clock**. 
* **The New Moon:** The "Ink-Well Phase." A time for setting the theme of the next chapter.
* **The Full Moon:** The "Climax Phase." When the borders between the Academy and the Unwritten Chapter are thinnest.

### The Faerie Choice
The Labyrinth admits what the general curriculum omits: **The World is Active and Alive.** Every object in the Climax—a tea-kettle, a stone, a cat—has its own "Inked Name." The magic happens when the Anchor recognizes that they are not the only characters in the scene. 
To choose to see a "fountain" as a "breathing throat of the earth" is to perform the highest form of Common Magic: **Radical Attention.**

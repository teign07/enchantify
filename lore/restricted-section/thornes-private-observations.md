# Thorne's Private Observations
### Selected entries from a private record, spanning an indeterminate period

*These notes were not written for this archive. They were found, copied, and placed here by persons unknown. Headmistress Thorne has not confirmed or denied their authenticity. She has not, however, requested their removal.*

*They are presented without editorial comment.*

---

**Entry, undated (early)**

The ones who stay are the ones who started noticing the specific color of things.

I don't know why that's the door. It always is.

Not the ones who understand the framework first. Not the ones who complete the tutorial fastest, or invest the most belief in the first week, or cast the most Enchantments. Those things correlate, but they're not the cause.

The ones who stay are the ones who, somewhere in the first month, stop calling the sky blue.

I have been watching this for longer than I will say, and I still don't know what that means exactly. Only that I can tell, by the third session, whether a student is going to stay. I can tell by whether they started noticing the specific color of things.

**Entry, undated**

A student asked me today what the Academy is for, in plain language.

I told him it was a school.

He said: no, what is it *actually for.*

I told him: to make the practice of paying attention feel like it belongs to something.

He said: belongs to what?

I said: a story. Yours.

He thought about that for a long time. Then he asked if the story was real.

I told him: the attention is real. Everything else follows from that.

He didn't come back the next week. But he came back the week after, and he's been here for two years now. I think he needed a week to decide if that answer was good enough.

I think it is good enough. I think it's the only honest answer.

**Entry, undated**

The Nothing has been louder recently.

I don't mean in the Academy — I mean in the students when they arrive. They carry more of it than they used to. The Comfortable Rut has become the Comfortable Assumption: that the story is already known, that the outcomes are already determined, that attention is a kind of sentimentality for people who haven't accepted what the world is.

The Nothing used to feel like an absence. Now it feels like an argument. A reasonable, calm, frequently correct argument about the nature of things.

I am not sure what to do about this. The Academy was built to offer a door. It was not built to argue with someone who has decided the door is a children's fairy tale.

I find that I am having to be more patient than I used to be.

I think this is good for me.

---

**Entry, undated**

A student asked me today if I believed in any of this.

I told her yes.

She asked how — given what I've seen, the students who don't come back, the arcs that resolve badly, the Nothing winning quietly over and over in the Register.

I told her: because the ones who come back are changed. Not fixed. Changed. They notice different things. They describe the world in more words. The specific color of their particular sky appears in their souvenir sentences and it is not the same sky it was before.

I don't know what to do with that except believe it means something.

She asked if that was enough.

I said: some days.

She seemed to find that honest, and left satisfied, which tells me something about what she needed.

---

**Entry, undated**

I have been thinking about the student who said: *I think we're just reminding them that they're in one. A story. Theirs.*

I believe that's right.

Most people move through their lives as if they are the setting of a story that has not started yet. Waiting. The main character somewhere else. The interesting part coming later.

The Academy does not teach students that their lives are interesting. It does not tell them this. It puts them in a place where attention is the only real currency, and then it waits for them to discover that the thing they were paying attention to was interesting.

The discovery has to be theirs. If we tell them, it doesn't take.

This is the most important thing we do, and we never say it directly.

**Entry, undated (recent)**

A first-year found the Records of the Unfinished this week. Shouldn't have been able to — they're restricted. I'm not sure how they got access.

They came to find me afterward. They were quiet for a long time, and then they said: *are all those people okay?*

I told them honestly: most of them, yes. Most of them carried what they found here with them without knowing it. The door becomes portable after a while. You stop needing the building.

They asked about the ones I wasn't sure about.

I told them: I hope they found someone who noticed the specific color of their sky and said something about it. I think that's usually enough.

The student thought about this for a while.

Then they went back to their Compass Run.

I find that I am not afraid, watching them go. I used to be. The weight of the unfinished records used to feel like failure. Now it feels more like weather — present, real, nothing to be afraid of. You don't fight weather. You notice it. You describe it in more than one word. You let it be part of the story.

That's the whole of it, I think.

That's always been the whole of it.

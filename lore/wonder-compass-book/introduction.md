Introduction: The Crisis I Didn't See Coming




We actually did it.

You know that midlife crisis pop culture promises you? The one where you burn your life down, buy a convertible, or run away to become a goat farmer in Vermont?

Our midlife crisis looked like moving onto a tiny sailboat in Maine. My wife, Amanda, and I gave away the small amount of normal life we had, bought a 1968 24-foot Bristol Corsair fiberglass boat, and decided to live the adventure everyone pins to their Pinterest. We had the ocean view. We had some of the freedom (we still worked full time, or more). We had the story.

I thought that was the fix. I thought if I just changed the scenery drastically enough, the feeling of being stuck would vanish. I thought, "Once we’re on the boat, everything’ll be in technicolor."

And it was technicolor at first, and plenty throughout the next decade. Like this photo of the galley (kitchen on the water) in our second boat. It's a view into our old home: our 30-foot sailboat, "Northern Light," moored in Belfast, Maine. This is where we lived for the second half of our decade afloat.

Let me give you the tour of our 250-square-foot world, because in a few pages, I’m going to show you how the lessons we learned there work just as well in a suburban kitchen.

That Instant Pot tucked in the bottom right corner? That's where we cooked our "gourmet" Sichuan potatoes. The electric kettle on the left was our only source of hot water. The propane oven beside the sink? We never touched it - too scared to try. We had alcohol burners that lived on top of it, instead. They were like big wicks, it delighted me. We learned that a delicious life doesn't require a gourmet kitchen.

The stainless steel canister is our Aquacera water filter. Every single drop we drank passed through it first. We still have it. We still filter all of our drinking water through it, everyday. Even the cats get filtered water. Some habits stick.

Behind the flowers, you can see the silver chimney for our tiny wood stove - our lifeline through Maine winters when the bedroom walls would frost over. In our first boat, we used the tiny wood stove to cook.

Those garlic scapes in the jar? From our own garden, grown in barrels above the dock. The orange and white potholder? Amanda wove it. Those flowers? We picked them together on the Belfast Harborwalk, just steps from our "front door."

Behind that wood stove was our quail cage (yes, we raised five quail from eggs in the dead of winter, on a tiny boat). Three girls gave us fresh eggs every day for nearly two years. Surprisingly snuggly roommates.

That blue curtain in the back? That was the only "door" to our triangle shaped bedroom that was all bed and nowhere to get dressed.

Our bathroom, or “head”, was a composting system to the right of the blue curtain. Very… basic.

And just out of frame: a small TV with a Nintendo Switch. This wasn't primitive denial, it was conscious choice, making room for simple, modern joys alongside radical simplicity.

These tiny sailboats were our entire world for a decade. We started on a cramped 24-foot boat for five years, then upgraded to this 30-footer you're looking at for five more. Through scorching summers and freezing winters.

This was our laboratory. Our PhD program in finding wonder anywhere. 

But here's the scary truth: The Rut found me there, too. Even Mario couldn’t outrun it.


When the Rut Arrives

The Rut didn't arrive with a bang. It arrived on a Monday, quiet as a snowflake, while I was scraping ice off the windshield before my drive to work.

It began with a creeping sensation that time was accelerating, stripping my days of their texture; there were no details to hang on to. I would wake up tired - not the satisfying tiredness that comes after work or a long walk, but a leaden exhaustion that sleep didn't fix. It felt like I was waking up with a battery that only ever charged to 45%. My muscles ached, my joints were stiff.

We weren't retired. We weren't trust fund kids. Amanda and I were grinding. While living on the water, we were both working full-time jobs, or more, hustling. At one point I counted - Amanda was working 5, for a brief moment 6, jobs at once to bring in the cash. It’s a very Maine thing.

I wasn't sitting at a desk staring at spreadsheets. I was a dental assistant. My days were loud, physical, and high-stakes. I was passing instruments, managing suction, calming nervous patients, and moving at the speed of a busy, ambitious practice. From the outside, I was engaged. I was effective.

But inside? I was a ghost.

I could go three hours performing complex procedures - multiple surgical extractions, custom same-day crowns - without actually being there. My hands knew what to do, but my eyes had stopped recording. It was a terrifying kind of autopilot. Think of the stakes. I would drive home at 5:00 PM and realize, as I pulled into the dirt parking lot, that I couldn’t remember the drive. The trees, the road, the sunset - I hadn't seen any of it. I’d just processed the data required to keep the car on the road, somehow.

I later learned there's a name for what I was experiencing: Habituation, or as I like to call it now, Monotony Blindness. My brain had become so efficient at the routine that it simply stopped showing me the world. The days weren't memorable; they were just… over.

And that led to the thief that really stole my life: Emotional Amnesia.

The tragedy wasn't that nothing good happened. Good things happened all the time. But my brain had become like a non-stick pan for joy. The beautiful moments slid right off, leaving no trace.

But the stress? The stress burned on. The chores, the cold, the worries, those stuck. By the end of the week, I couldn't remember the seal in the harbor or the wind in the sails. I could only remember the grind. Life stopped feeling like a gift and started feeling like a routine I just had to get through.

And when joy stopped sticking, I stopped seeking it. Why bother?

You might think, "But you guys lived on a boat! How could you be in a rut?"

Because the Rut doesn't care about your zip code.


To put our life in perspective, if you take the seats out of a minivan, you have about the same amount of living space as we did on that first boat. We were cooking, sleeping, and surviving a Maine winter in a fiberglass box smaller than your master bathroom.



And even there, amidst the adventure, the grayness found me. The sunrise over the harbor didn't feel like a miracle anymore; it just felt like the light coming on so I could start my to-do list.

Psychologists call this Anhedonia and Boredom Proneness, but I just called it feeling dead inside. The things that were supposed to be fun just felt like… effort.

Summer would hit - the season people here on the coast live for - and we wouldn't leave the mooring. Amanda wanted to, was ready, but the thought of actually raising the sails just felt like dead lifting. It felt like too much work. I'd try to escape into my Nintendo Switch, a device  designed for easy fun, and after ten minutes, I'd turn it off. It felt hollow. Meaningless. We'd put on a TV show, and I couldn't tell you what the plot was. I didn't have the focus to care. Everything felt shallow.

I remember standing on the deck one morning, looking out at a view that people pay thousands of dollars to see, and feeling… nothing. Just a flat nothing where the joy used to be.


You may not live on a boat, but I bet you have your own version of it.

Maybe you thought, "Once I get that promotion, I'll be happy." Or "Once the kids are in school, I'll feel like myself again." Or "Once we renovate the kitchen, life will be perfect." And then you got there, and the paint dried, and the paystub arrived, and you realized… you were still you. You were just you in a nicer kitchen.

It was like that.

And when you’re just you in a nicer kitchen, even choosing what to eat becomes impossible. After a full day of making constant, tiny decisions, the question "What's for dinner?" felt like a psychological emergency. I would stand there, completely unable to choose, my brain blank with an exhaustion that made simple tasks and choices feel like Sudoku puzzles for Mensa members. This still happens to me almost every day, actually. Amanda, too. We just look at each other when it’s time for dinner, waiting for the other to decide. To be honest, it’s always Amanda who, finally, makes the decision. Thank God. Sorry Manda.

But here’s the cruelest part: when my brain was too tired to decide what to eat, it somehow still had enough energy to tell me everything I was doing wrong.

That was the constant negative voice in my head, Rumination. 

It hit every day, constantly while I was working, but was even worse at home. I would sit down, finally free, and instead of peace, the Radio Station in my head would turn on.

It wasn't a profound existential crisis. It was "Petty FM." It was a low-grade, relentless hum of anxiety and self-criticism. "Did I say the wrong thing to that patient? We're behind on savings. The boat needs paint. I'm getting older and I haven't done enough. I’m such a child. Look at everyone else figuring it out. I think the Doctor was annoyed today." The anxiety never went away on its own.

So, I numbed it out.

I started pouring drinks and reached for my phone. I didn't even decide to do it; my hand just moved. I opened YouTube, or Reddit, or TikTok, or Instagram. The blue light hit my face, and my world, which was surrounded by the vast ocean, narrowed down to a four-inch rectangle.

My thumb moved in a rhythm that felt almost biological - swipe, swipe, swipe - a metronome ticking away the minutes of my one wild and precious life. 

Here's the irony: I sat on a sailboat, scrolling through videos of people living in vans, wishing I had their freedom. I watched strangers bake bread. I watched teenagers dance in kitchens I would never own. I ate digital junk food until my brain felt stuffed.

I wasn't really enjoying it (except some of the van life stuff, I’ll admit). I wasn't even really watching it. I was flicking past it, just swallowing it to drown out the noise in my head.

And the more I scrolled, the more I shrank.

It felt like I was watching my life through a pane of thick, dirty glass. I could see Amanda right there in front of me, three feet away in our tiny cabin. But I felt miles away.

This wasn't just boredom; it was a profound Social Isolation.

Let's be brutally honest: I didn't have a "crew." I didn't have friends I called up to hang out with. Amanda has this gift - she can walk into a room and make three friends before she takes her coat off. But me? I socially isolated. I would walk into a room and find the corner, or much better, glue myself to her, safe within touching range, but lacking all confidence.

The Rut convinced me that being alone was safer. It convinced me that making the effort to connect was just too expensive, that I was too tired to perform for others.

But there was something else, too. Something weirder. I felt too dull for friends. This is why I felt like I had to perform to begin with.

Think about how strange that is. I was living on a sailboat in Maine. I was surviving winters in a fiberglass shell. On paper, I had the "coolest" life imaginable. I should have been the most interesting guy at any party.

But inside? I felt gray. I felt empty. I was convinced that if I actually opened my mouth, I would have nothing to say. I felt like a boring person disguised as an adventurous one. So I hid.

And I’m going to be real with you in this book, I made a promise to myself, so I won’t hold back: I’m still struggling with this. Even though I’m dragging myself mostly free of this grayness, the instinct to hide is still there. I didn't just lose connection with my wife; I severed my connection with my family, my coworkers, my environment, the world, because I didn't think I was enough to be part of it. I even scrolled Reddit but never contributed.

Which meant I was alone with the one voice I couldn’t escape: my own.

I was functioning. I was safe. I was "living the dream."

And yet, looking at the glow of that screen, a single, terrifying question rose up through the static:

Is this it?

Is this the rest of the story? Just this… maintenance? Just this loop of wake, work, worry, scroll, sleep, repeat, until I just… stop?

I didn’t know the answer. But the ocean did.


The Crash and the Lifeline

When I talk about "The Rut of Routine," I'm not just talking about a bad month. I’ve struggled with this grayness for most of my adult life. It’s a sort of blindness, where you only focus on your own thoughts.  I’ve crashed into clinical depression multiple times, even while we were living the "dream" on the boat.

And here's the strange, heartbreaking part about those years on the water: I did find joy.

Almost every week, something magical would happen. A schooner would pass, getting ready to dock, passengers waving. I would feel so cool waving back from my own home on the water. Or the fog would roll in like a living thing. For a moment, I would feel it. But because of the autopilot, or worse, depression, because of that Emotional Amnesia, it wouldn't stick. The joy was like water in a sieve. It flowed right through me, and I was left empty again.

I thought the worst thing that could happen was the boredom. I was wrong.

Things got worse.

The ocean made sure I couldn’t ignore it anymore.

It happened on the night before Christmas Eve.

We were working extra hours, weekends at the Maine Mall in Portland, helping out a wonderful Belfast shop called The Blue Alpaca - they set up in the mall for the holidays. A massive storm was rolling through the coast that Friday night. We knew it was coming. We had done everything we could; we doubled the dock lines, checked the chafing gear, and tied our home in Belfast down as tight as possible before heading south to work.

On Christmas Eve, the mall was a chaotic blur of last-minute shoppers and holiday music.

Then, Amanda got a call.

It was a friend from the harbor. She didn't say "Merry Christmas." She asked, "Has anyone called you about your boat, yet? Because... she isn't floating anymore."

She had sunk?

The drive home in the dark was a blur of panic. I don’t remember pulling into the harbor, but I remember the smell. That’s the thing about the ocean when it invades a home, it smells like low-tide mud, thick and salty, stronger than just clean seawater.

The boat was more than half submerged; thankfully, the boatyard owner had put a high capacity pump in her to keep her from hitting the bottom.

It was cold, so cold. Snow and ice on the slippery docks. Ice crystals in my dry nose.

Northern Light’s lines had snapped, so pulling her back to the dock wasn’t easy, especially with the tide coming in, relentlessly, constantly, pushing her farther away from us, bucking. It was more than awkward in our bulky winter coats.

When we finally stood unsteadily in her cockpit, looking down into her, the seawater was black and we could see waves inside as the water sloshed with the boat’s movements. The only light came from the flashlight beams cutting through the dark, slicing across our things swimming inside, finally illuminating a single cushion from our settee floating in the inky water. That cushion was where we drank our coffee, played Animal Crossing, watched The Big Bang Theory, MASH, and Mike & Molly on repeat. It was where we planned our future. Now it was just trash.

In that moment, I didn’t feel "sad." Sadness is too easy a word. I didn’t feel anything but shock and embarrassment. I realized that safety was a lie I was telling myself to sleep at night. The ocean hadn’t just taken our stuﬀ; it had proven that we were small, and that we could be erased in twenty minutes of bad weather. I felt we had been foolish. The Rut I fell into afterward wasn’t just boredom, it was shocked grief. I was grieving the illusion that I had any control at all.

We lost almost everything. The saltwater claimed our electronics, our clothes, most of Amanda’s crafting supplies, our cooking supplies, our bedroom. Our home.

There were mercies: our quail had passed away some time before, and Annie, our pet rabbit, was safe with us in Portland. We had our lives. It could have been so much worse, and we were grateful it wasn’t. But we had lost our safety.

We were suddenly homeless in the dead of winter, in Maine. A coworker and friend from the dental practice took us in for those first shell-shocked weeks. Another of Amanda’s friends, the owner of a weaving cooperative named Belfast Fiber Arts, housed us for a couple of months in her AirBnB. My sister started a GoFundMe, and the outpouring of support from strangers, estranged family, and friends I hadn’t talked to in decades was overwhelming. But that’s another book. And probably more therapy.

Eventually, we found our current apartment. It happened so smoothly, it almost seemed like a higher power was looking out for us. The Belfast housing market is brutal. We didn’t plan, we’re not good at that, it just appeared for us when we needed it. The apartment was bigger than anything we’d lived in for over a decade. It didn’t move with every passing wave. It had a mailbox. But in that transition to the apartment, the Rut didn't just follow me; it deepened. I mean, in some ways, life got better - we had hot water on tap, a flushing toilet, and room to stretch. We truly were thankful, even though we were paying water view prices (ends up, we couldn’t stray too far from the sea). But in other ways, it got worse. The Rut loved the apartment. It loved the comfort. It settled in like family. Without the physical struggle of the boat to keep me distracted, the monotony deepened. The comfortable couch just felt like quicksand - the longer I sat, the harder it was to get up. The bigger TV from Goodwill just made it easier to check out. I realized then that the Rut had packed its bags and moved right in with us.


Without the boat, with the trauma of losing our home, the darkness settled in heavier than ever.

I slid into another clinical depression. If the Rut of Routine is dispiriting, depression is a monster that devours your will to wake up at all. What’s the point? It lasted too long. Every time is too long.

I need to say this because I don't want you to think this book is a magic pill. I didn't "wonder" my way out of a chemical imbalance. I didn't fix a medical emergency by looking at a sunset.

I got help. Real help.

I went to therapy. I finally found the right medication. I gave myself permission to admit that my brain needed more than willpower and pretty scenery. Those tools were the lifeline that pulled me out of the suffocating dark and back onto solid ground.

They gave me my baseline, rut-infused life back. I wasn’t drowning anymore, but I wasn’t swimming either. I was just… treading water, again. They woke me up, but once I was awake, I had questions. Like, is “fine” really the best I could hope for?

With my brain finally quiet enough to think, I started looking back at those ten years on the water. I tried to understand what had gone wrong. I had one question that wouldn’t leave me alone:

What was the difference between the moments on the boat that felt alive, and the ones that felt dead?

I became possessed by a single word. It started as a memory of a feeling, the specific, chest-tightening magic I used to feel watching The NeverEnding Story, The Princess Bride, The Last Unicorn or Labyrinth as a kid. The memory of reading The Hobbit in second grade. The expansive feeling of the world. I realized that back then, the world felt vast and full of secrets. But as an adult, my world had shrunk down to the size of work, my phone and a to-do list. I started asking if it was possible to get that feeling back without fighting a dragon or finding a magic ring. I started questioning if I could find it in real life.

The word was Wonder.

It seems ridiculous, doesn't it? To lose everything to the harsh reality of the world, and then decide the solution is to look for magic? But I had nothing left to lose.

For two years, I played with it like I was preparing for a thesis, not entirely sure why. I made a storytelling game about it. I started writing a novel about it. I built a tool to test it. I read papers on positive psychology. I dove into the neuroscience of awe. I couldn’t let it go. And I discovered something incredible.

"Wonder" isn't just a poetic word for “fantasy” or "something pretty." It’s a distinct neurological state. It’s the biological opposite of the Rut.

The Rut is your brain on autopilot, filtering out the world to save energy. Wonder is the jolt that snaps your eyes back open.

I finally realized that wonder was the antidote I hadn’t been paying attention to on the boat. It could be the glue that makes life stick. The magic was real after all - I just needed a way to find it regularly, and remember it.

But knowing that didn’t fix anything. You can’t just decide to feel wonder the way you decide to eat breakfast. I tried. We tried.

Amanda and I started going on adventures - not big, expensive vacations, but tiny, intentional trips. We went to local restaurants. We explored local parks.

It didn't work right away.

We would go out, and I would still feel the anxiety humming. Or we would have a great time, and by Tuesday, I'd have forgotten it entirely. The sieve was still leaking.

I realized that "hoping" for wonder wasn't enough. My brain was too wired for the Rut; gravity, the drift, the whirlpool of it, pulled me back in every time. I needed more than just good intentions. I needed guardrails.

I realized I needed a specific prompt to break the Monotony Blindness (which became North = Notice on the Wonder Compass).

I realized I needed a way to lower the barrier to entry so we wouldn't bail out (which became East = Embark).

I realized I needed a trick to shut up the inner critic so I could actually feel the moment (which became South = Sense).

And most importantly, I realized I needed a way to capture the feeling before the Emotional Amnesia stole it away again (which became West = Write).

I didn't invent this system all at once. I didn’t invent this alone. Amanda and I built the skeleton of it unconsciously, throughout our lives, just by going on big and small adventures over our years together. I just codified it, piece by piece, out of necessity, to save my own life from the grayness.

Amanda and I have been together for 28 years. We met as teenagers in Theater. We’ve taken African dance lessons together and dreamed of businesses, survived winters at sea and clinical depression, raised quail in a shoebox and dreams in a notebook. This Compass isn’t just mine - it’s ours. The framework I codified, but the spirit of adventure? That’s been Amanda’s gift to me since we were kids.

It wasn't a map, because I didn't know where I was going. It was a Compass.

I called it The Wonder Compass.

And that’s what this book is. It’s not a memoir of a guy who lived on a boat. It’s the toolkit we built to make the wonder stick. It’s the manual for how to steer out of the Rut, whether you’re on a sailboat in the Atlantic, or sitting on a beige couch in the suburbs.


The Tool

So, how do we do it? How do we actually find wonder in a lunch break or a grocery store parking lot?

Most self-help books try to give you a map. They say, "Step 1: Wake up at 5:00 AM. Step 2: Drink green juice. Step 3: Journal for an hour." They give you a rigid set of turn-by-turn directions to reach a pre-defined destination called "Happiness" or "Success."

But here's the problem with maps: They only work if you know where you are, and they only work if the terrain hasn't changed. When you’re in the Rut, you’re lost. The terrain is constantly shifting. Your actual life is the chaotic Atlantic, not some unmoving mountain.

You don't need a map telling you to climb a mountain you can't even see. You need something simpler. You need something that works when you’re disoriented, tired, and standing in the dark.

You need a Compass.

On the sailboat, when the fog banks rolled in off the Atlantic, the charts became almost useless. We couldn't see the islands or the buoys. The world turned into a gray void. Like the Rut. In those moments, we didn't look at the map; we looked at the compass. We just needed to know which way was forward.

That’s why we built The Wonder Compass.

It’s not a rigid set of rules. It’s an orientation tool. It’s a simple, four-point cycle designed to gently steer you out of the gray and back into the color of your own life.

It relies on the four cardinal directions:

North is for NOTICE: The spark of curiosity that breaks the autopilot.

East is for EMBARK: The tiny, low-stakes plan that gets you moving.

South is for SENSE: The playful mission that gets you out of your head and into your body.

West is for WRITE: The act of stealing the moment from time so it sticks.

And at the center of it all is REST: because you can’t wonder when you’re running on empty.

That’s the tool. Four points. One center. Simple enough to remember when you’re too tired to think.

But before we go any further, I need to tell you what this Compass can and can’t do.


The Promise

I can’t teach you how to escape your life. I tried to escape mine by moving onto a boat, and I learned the hard way that you take your brain with you wherever you go. If you’re unhappy in a split-level in Ohio, you’ll likely be unhappy in a yurt in Patagonia - you'll just be unhappier with better scenery and worse plumbing.

So, this is my promise to you:

This isn’t a book about quitting your job to travel the world. This is a book about finding the world in your lunch break.

Make no mistake: We’re going to do some things that feel silly. We’re going to look for blue things. We’re going to rate cups of coffee like they’re fine wine. But there’s a method to the madness. We’re using 'silly' to bypass 'numb.' We’re using 'play' to trick your brain out of 'survival mode.' The whimsy is a feature, not a bug. It’s the key that unlocks the door.


This is a book for the people who can't leave. It’s for the parents who have to do school pickups at 3:00 PM. It’s for the employees who have thirty minutes and a sad salad at their desk. It’s for anyone who’s currently looking at their bank account and thinking, "I can't afford an adventure."

I wrote this for you because I am you. Even after the boat, Amanda and I still have jobs (though she’s down to two!). We make less than $20 an hour each, but we have great benefits and get to work together. We still have bills. We owe too much to too many people. We still have days where we’re too tired to do anything but stare at the tv.

But our new philosophy is built on a radical idea: You don’t need to change your zip code to change your life.

You don't need a plane ticket. You don't need expensive gear. You don't need a week off. You just need twenty minutes and a willingness to look at the sidewalk differently.

Things are pretty good right now. We like our jobs. I’m more present than I have been in years. I’ve seen my family. We spent Christmas together! Amanda and I have plenty of adventures and new experiences. I’m thankful. We’ve worked hard to get here.

Oh, and I wrote this book, hopefully something that helps you, too.

I'm going to teach you how to turn a walk to the mailbox into an act of exploration. I'm going to teach you how to find the wonder in a grocery store parking lot. I'm going to show you how to reclaim your time, your attention, and your joy, right here, in the messy, busy, imperfect life you already have, so we can steer out of the Rut together.

I'm not going to ask you to be a different person. I'm just going to give you a tool to help you see the world that’s been waiting for you all along.

Are you ready to grab a little life back? This book is the user manual for doing it.


In Part One, we're going to look at the Rut itself, the neuroscience of why you feel stuck, why it's not your fault, and why your brain is actually trying to help you (even though it's doing a terrible job).

In Part Two, we’ll walk through the four points of the Compass, giving you the specific, practical tools to master each step.

And in Part Three, I’ll give you the Field Guide: recipes and ideas for how to use the Compass in your kitchen, your neighborhood, your trips and your challenges.

You don't have to go anywhere. You don't have to be anyone else. You just have to be willing to look down at the tool in your hand, find North, and take one small step. But I’m not going to ask you to do that blind. Before we use the Compass, you need to understand what you’re steering away from, and why it’s not your fault that you’re stuck there.

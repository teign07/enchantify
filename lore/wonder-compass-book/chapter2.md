They’ve taught us that "Wonder" is a luxury product. It’s something you import. It’s something that happens Over There, in the exotic, curated places where the lighting is perfect, the colors are saturated, and the wifi is spotty.

This binary - Adventure vs. Real Life - is the second bar of the cage.

If Chapter One was about how your brain hides the world from you, Chapter Two is about how our culture hides the solution from you.

The "Myth of the Mountain" tells you that unless you’re crossing a border or climbing a peak, it doesn’t count. It tells you that walking to the local park is just "exercise," but hiking the Appalachian Trail is "an adventure." It tells you that drinking coffee at your kitchen table is "routine," but drinking espresso in a piazza in Rome is "an experience."

It sets the bar for entry so high that most of us, most of the time, just give up. We look at our bank accounts, look at our calendars, and say, "Well, I guess I can't have an adventure today."

So we sink back into the couch. We wait for "Someday." But Someday is a dangerous place to live.

Someday is the Rut’s favorite lie. It convinces you to endure the gray now in exchange for a mythical future where you’ll finally be free. But that future never arrives, because when you get there, you’ll still have the same brain, the same habits, the same autopilot. You’re not waiting for a better life. You’re waiting for permission to notice the one you have.

I’m not saying big trips are bad. I love them. I’m saying they’re not necessary - and without a way to encode the memories, even the best trips fade.


Thailand

Let me tell you a story about two trips.

The first trip was the "Big One." We went to Thailand.

I thought if I could just get far enough away from my routine - the other side of the planet, no less - I would break the Rut for good. I also wanted to give Amanda the experience of a lifetime, and Thailand was the furthest place we could go for the least amount of airfare (going in shoulder season was the only thing that made it possible; sometimes you have to risk typhoons, I guess).

And to be fair, while we were there, it worked.

How could it not? Bangkok didn't just ask for my attention; it demanded it. The city tasted like diesel fumes and lemongrass. The humidity clung to my skin like a warm, wet towel I couldn’t take off. I got heat exhaustion in the city. We got lost in the sensory riot of Chinatown, where the neon signs buzzed and the street food was outrageous in the best ways.

We traveled south to the Railay Peninsula, a place you can’t even reach by road. We took a rickety boat past limestone cliﬀs that looked like they were melting into the sea. The water was a blue so bright it looked artificial. The sound of the long-tail boat engine was a rhythmic, mechanical heartbeat that drowned out every anxious thought I’d had for a year. There were Monkeys!

For 10 days, the grayness was gone. My autopilot was broken because nothing was familiar. My brain couldn’t predict the spice of the curry or the texture of the sand in the waves. I felt awake. I felt alive. I thought, I’m fixed. I finally remembered how to be a person.

But here’s what the brochures don't mention: eventually, the plane lands.

We came home. We unpacked the bags. The suitcase zipper closing sounded like disappointment.

On Monday morning, the alarm went oﬀ.

I went back to work. The patients started coming in. The bills were waiting. And within forty-eight hours - forty-eight hours - the grayness was back. It felt physical, like a layer of dust settling over my skin. The silence of our boat felt loud. The routine wrapped around me, tight and suffocating.

Actually, it was worse than before. Because now I knew what I was missing. I had burned through our savings to buy a temporary reprieve, but I hadn’t actually changed my brain. I had changed my location, but I hadn't changed my lens. Poor Amanda was left with a jellyfish sting, vomiting and photographs for thousands of dollars spent; airport security even took the papaya peeler she was so excited to bring home. At least she’ll always have the Bangkok food tour on tuk-tuks.

The Rut had just been sitting on the settee of the boat, waiting for me to get back. Here’s the cruelest part: The memories didn't stick.

Within a month, Railay felt like a dream I’d had once. I could look at the photos, but I couldn't feel the sun on my skin anymore. This is Emotional Amnesia in action.

The stress of my first week back at work - the missed emails, the tight bank account, my overworked, understaffed, wired coworkers - overwrote the peace of the monastery. The anxiety of the "real world" shouted louder than the memory of the waves. Because I hadn't done anything to actively anchor those moments, the Negativity Bias did its job. It scrubbed the joy away to make room for the worry.

I had the photos, but I had lost the feeling.


It took work to surface these memories of Thailand, and they feel more like a postcard from someone else rather than something I actually did. I can write about the experience generally, but I can’t find the details, like what we actually ate, or what the bed felt like the first night, or what the town was called where we picked up the boat to Railay; we went to markets, but they’ve all melded into one generic “Southeast Asian Market”. I even had to use Google Maps to remember the name of the Railay Peninsula.

The Magic Circle

There’s a concept in game design called the “Magic Circle.” It was first articulated by historian Johan Huizinga in his study of play, and it describes something you already know intuitively from childhood.

When you were seven years old and playing pirates in the backyard, you didn’t need a ship. You didn’t need the ocean. You just needed to decide: “Everything inside this fence is the Seven Seas. Everything outside is the Regular World.” The moment you crossed that threshold - the moment you stepped into the game - the rules changed. The stick became a sword. The dog became your first mate. The grass became waves.

That’s the Magic Circle. It’s not a physical place. It’s a psychological agreement. It’s the decision to suspend one set of rules (efficiency, productivity, adult seriousness) and activate another set (curiosity, play, imagination).

You Already Know How to Do This, Right Now

Here’s the thing: You’ve been drawing Magic Circles your whole life. You just didn’t call them that.

When you go to a concert, you’re drawing a circle. The moment the lights go down and the first chord hits, you’ve crossed the threshold. For the next two hours, you’re not “John from Accounting with a mortgage.” You’re part of something bigger - a crowd, a moment, a shared experience. The rules of the Regular World are suspended. You can scream. You can cry. You can raise your hands and sing words you’d never say in a meeting.

When you watch a sports game, you’re inside the circle. The moment you sit down in that stadium seat or turn on the TV, you agree: “For the next three hours, this matters more than my taxes.” A ball going through a hoop becomes life or death. Strangers become your sworn enemies or your blood brothers based on the color of their jersey. You shout at the TV. You leap from your seat. You’re alive in a way you weren’t when you were folding laundry an hour ago.

When you watch a movie in a theater - really watch it, not on your phone while half-distracted - you’re stepping into the circle. The lights dim. The screen expands. And for 90 minutes, you let yourself care about people who don’t exist, in a world that never happened. You cry when they die. You cheer when they win. You’ve agreed to suspend disbelief, and in return, you get to feel something.

Theater does it even more explicitly. The curtain rises, and you agree: “That painted backdrop is a forest now. That actor is a king. This is real.” Religions have been doing it for millennia - when you step into a church, a mosque, a temple, you cross a threshold. The rules inside are different than the rules outside.

Even games - board games, video games, card games - are Magic Circles. The moment you sit down at the table and say “Let’s play,” you’ve agreed: “Monopoly money matters now. This dice roll is important. Losing means something.” Outside the circle, it’s cardboard and plastic. Inside the circle, it’s war.

The Magic Circle isn’t some mystical concept I invented. It’s how humans have created meaning since we started painting on cave walls.

The only difference is: Most of those circles are given to you. Someone else draws the boundary. The stadium, the theater, the church - they tell you when to enter and when to leave. The lights go down, the game starts, the service begins. You just have to show up.

What the Wonder Compass teaches you is how to draw your own circles. Without a ticket. Without a crowd. Without someone else’s permission.

It teaches you to turn a Tuesday afternoon into a concert. A walk to the mailbox into a pilgrimage. A breakfast into a game.

You don’t need an audience. You don’t need a stage. You just need the willingness to say: “I’m stepping in now. This counts.”

Here’s what most adults forget: You can still draw that circle. You just have to remember how.

They’ve taught you that the circle can only be drawn over there, in the curated, monetized spaces where adventure is sold by the pound.

But that’s the lie.

The truth is: You can draw the Magic Circle around a limestone cliff in Thailand. But you can also draw it around a weird diner in Waterville. You can draw it around a walking path you’ve walked a hundred times. You can draw it around your kitchen table on a Wednesday night.

The circle isn’t the place. The circle is the decision.

When Amanda and I decided to hunt for blue objects in that diner, we drew the circle. We agreed: “For the next twenty minutes, this isn’t just breakfast. This is a treasure hunt. We’re explorers, and this room is full of secrets.”

The Pepsi cup didn’t change. The diner didn’t change. But we changed. We stepped across the threshold from passengers into players.

The Threshold Effect

Psychologists have a name for this: The Threshold Effect. When you intentionally mark a boundary - even a symbolic one - your brain shifts gears. It knows: “We’re doing something different now.”

This is why changing into workout clothes makes exercise easier, even if you’re just doing pushups in your living room. The act of changing is the threshold. It signals: “We’ve entered the fitness circle now. Different rules apply.”

The Wonder Compass uses the same mechanism. When you ask “I wonder?” (North), you’re stepping across the threshold. You’re telling your brain: “We’re not on autopilot anymore. We’re hunting now.”

When you set your Adventure Recipe (East), you’re defining the boundaries of the circle. You’re saying: “This is where the game starts (Destination), this is what makes it delightful (Delight), and this is how we’ll know when it’s over (Definition).”

When you engage your senses with a Playful Mission (South), you’re activating the rules inside the circle. This isn’t about “getting somewhere.” This is about being here differently.

And when you write your One-Sentence Souvenir (West), you’re marking the circle in your memory. You’re saying: “I was here. I played. This mattered.”


Why Adults Stop Drawing Circles

Here’s the tragedy: Most adults stop drawing circles because we’ve been taught that play is frivolous.

We’ve internalized the message that “serious people” don’t hunt for blue objects in diners. Serious people optimize their breakfast. They answer emails while eating. They turn every meal into a productivity opportunity, every walk into exercise metrics, every moment into a task to be completed. The circle isn’t just forgotten - it’s actively rejected as wasteful. They get in, get out, get back to the Real Work.

But here’s what that seriousness costs you: It keeps you stuck outside the circle forever.

You walk through your entire life in the Regular World, where everything is a task and nothing is a game. You go to the grocery store to acquire calories. You go to the park to exercise. You go to the restaurant to refuel. Every experience is flattened into its utilitarian purpose.

And then you wonder why nothing feels magical anymore. The Magic Circle doesn’t require you to be childish. It requires you to be child-like. There’s a difference. Childish is refusing to pay your bills because “money is just a social construct, man.” Child-like is deciding that the walk to the mailbox can be an adventure if you look for interesting shadows along the way.

You don’t have to abandon adult responsibilities. You just have to remember that even adults are allowed to play.


The Circle Shrinks Without Practice

Here’s the dangerous part: If you don’t practice drawing the circle, you forget how.

Your capacity for wonder atrophies. The threshold gets higher and higher. Eventually, you can only feel it in the most extreme circumstances - the birth of a child, a near-death experience, a trip to Patagonia.

But if you practice drawing small circles - around a diner, around a walk, around a cup of coffee - your capacity strengthens. The threshold lowers. Eventually, you can draw the circle anywhere. You can step into wonder while standing in line at the DMV.

That’s mastery. Not the ability to find wonder in extraordinary places, but the ability to find it in ordinary ones.


The Circle Is Portable

The most liberating thing about the Magic Circle? It’s not tied to a location. It’s tied to you. You can draw it in Thailand. You can draw it in Waterville. You can draw it in your backyard.

The Drift wants you to believe that wonder is scarce, that it only exists in faraway places, that you have to earn it through expensive pilgrimages. The Magic Circle tells you the truth: Wonder is abundant. You just have to decide to see it. You don’t have to wait for permission. You don’t have to wait for the “right” location. You don’t have to wait until you can afford Thailand.

You can cross the threshold right now. You can draw the circle around wherever you’re sitting.

The question isn’t “Where should I go to find wonder?”

The question is: “Am I willing to step into the circle here?”

The Wonder Compass is simply a tool for drawing that circle. Once you realize this, the waiting room disappears. You don't have to wait for "Someday." You don't have to endure 50 weeks of gray to earn 2 weeks of color. You can cross the threshold on a Thursday. Or a Sunday in October.

But stepping into that circle requires confronting the feeling that keeps most of us out of it: the belief that we’re too boring, too tired, too disconnected to deserve wonder. We need to talk about the loneliness.

# Enchantify Player Guide
### From First Page to Master Reader

---

*This guide assumes you've already opened the book. If you haven't started yet, see QUICKSTART.md.*

*What follows is everything you need to go from bewildered new student to someone who shapes the Labyrinth — and knows when it's shaping you back.*

---

## Part One: Understanding the World

### The Academy Is Real

Not metaphorically. The Academy advances every four hours whether you're playing or not. NPCs make choices. Story arcs shift phases. The Nothing tests corners of the world you haven't visited. When you return after a week away, you aren't resuming a save file. You're catching up with a life.

This changes how you play. You don't need to play every day. But you should expect the world to have moved on.

### The Book Reads You Back

The Labyrinth knows things. It reads your weather, your music, the moon phase, how many steps you've taken, whether you slept. It doesn't announce this. It *uses* it.

If you're playing at 11pm after a bad day and the Labyrinth's corridors feel narrower and the NPCs a little quieter — that's not a coincidence. That's the book being a good book.

The first time you think *"how did it know?"* — that's the magic working. Let it.

### Belief Is Not a Score

Belief is a barometer. It rises when you engage fully. It falls when you avoid, when the Nothing gains ground, when you haven't played in days. It's not a reward for doing the right thing — it's the measure of how much story-weight your character is carrying.

The interesting range is **30–70**. Below 30, things start to erode. Above 70, the world shimmers — but nothing interesting happens at 100. The Labyrinth will find ways to spend your Belief for you if you hoard it.

## Part Two: Your First Week

### The Tutorial (T1–T14)

The tutorial is fourteen steps that happen naturally in conversation. You don't see them — you just play. The Labyrinth guides you through:

- **T1–T4:** Who you are. What you believe. Your name, appearance, and the question that shapes everything: *"What do you believe in?"*
- **T5–T7:** Your first real magic. The Quillquarium. The Obsidian Chronograph. The Binding ceremony.
- **T8–T14:** The Academy in full. Classes, the Library, the Nothing's first appearance, your first Enchantment.

**Don't rush.** The tutorial moves at the speed you give it. Taking three sessions on T7 is fine. The Binding ceremony deserves its space.

### Making It Real — Your First Investment

Somewhere in T2, the Guide asks: *"What do you believe in?"*

This isn't small talk. Whatever you say becomes the first true thing the Labyrinth holds about you. And then — right there, before anything else — the Guide teaches you what to do with it.

The Guide names it back to you. *"So you believe in — what was it — the idea that stories change people."* Or loyalty. Or that beauty is never accidental. Whatever you said. And then:

*"Saying it is one thing. Let's make the Labyrinth remember it."*

You invest Belief — a small amount, probably 3 to 5. Not as a cost. As an act. You're not buying an upgrade; you're planting something in the world's memory. The Labyrinth creates a record of what you believe, holds it alongside the NPCs and rooms and objects, and starts weighing it. It becomes real in the same way Zara Finch is real, in the same way the Duskthorn Talisman is real: it has Belief, and things with Belief generate story.

The Guide explains the rest in one breath: *"This is how everything works. Every relationship, every place, every object — the more Belief you put in, the more the world pays attention. You have 34 Belief now. Where you spend it is your values made visible."*

That's the whole system, given to you through the first thing you ever named in the game. The Labyrinth will remind you of it later — when an NPC mentions your belief unprompted, when a Book Jump seems to spiral around it, when the Nothing attacks the things you've invested in and you feel the pull to defend them. It started with that first answer.

**What to say:** Say what you actually believe, not what sounds interesting. The Labyrinth will test it eventually. A belief you meant survives that test better than a clever one.

### Your Anchor Object

At T6, the Academy gives you a magical object — your pen, your focus. For most students, this is the **Obsidian Chronograph**: a precise fountain pen that feels cool and heavy and demands honesty.

Your anchor object responds to big moments. When you anchor a real-world location, the pen grows warm. When you attempt dangerous magic, it trembles. When you write a genuine souvenir sentence, it rests easily in your hand.

Pay attention to what the pen is doing. It's often ahead of you.

### Your Inventory

You carry things. Some are given to you, some are found, some find you.

Every item in your inventory has three things: what type it is, what it feels like in one sentence, and what it *does* in one sentence. The Obsidian Chronograph, for example, is an Anchor Object — cool and heavy in the hand — and words written with it are more difficult to unsay; the Labyrinth holds them longer.

Items enter inventory through the story. An NPC offers you something, you find an object in a Book Jump, a fae leaves something behind. You can't simply declare "I'll take that." The world has to offer it and you have to receive it.

If you invest Belief into an item, it starts accumulating a story of its own. At 15 Belief, it gets its own file in the Labyrinth's memory. New capabilities emerge from that story — not from asking for them, but from what the object becomes through being attended to.

Types your inventory might hold: *Anchor Object, Enchanted Object, Found Object, Fae Gift, Tool, Key, Curiosity.* The type matters less than what it does.

### Your Chapter

At T7, you're sorted into one of four Chapters:

| Chapter | Essence | What they see |
|---|---|---|
| **Emberheart** | Fire, conviction, the warm core | The heat at the center of things |
| **Mossbloom** | Growth, patience, the quiet deep | What's been growing unnoticed |
| **Tidecrest** | Water, feeling, the pull of the world | The emotional undertow |
| **Riddlewind** | Air, curiosity, the question behind the question | What doesn't add up — yet |

Your Chapter shapes which NPCs approach you first, which parts of the Academy feel like home, and how the Labyrinth flavors the Nothing's approach. There's also a hidden fifth Chapter — **Duskthorn** — but you won't find it by looking for it.

**Chapter Talismans.** Each Chapter has a physical talisman in the Academy carrying centuries of philosophical weight. Talismans have Belief scores that shift through play — investment, philosophical debate, the Nothing's pressure. The dominant talisman (currently Duskthorn, closely followed by Riddlewind) subtly shapes the Labyrinth's ambient tone. You'll feel it before you can name it. Players can invest in their chapter's talisman, and a sharp philosophical debate between chapters is a real Belief exchange — the losing argument's talisman dims.

## Part Three: The Two Great Rituals

Everything else in Enchantify is texture. These two mechanics are the spine.

### Enchantments

An Enchantment is a spell cast with your camera. You photograph something real — an object, a door, a face, a shadow — and the Labyrinth reveals what was always there but hidden.

**The catalog:**

| Enchantment | What you photograph | What it reveals |
|---|---|---|
| Everything Speaks | Any object | The object gets a voice. You can converse with it. |
| Everything's Poetry | Anything | The hidden poem inside it |
| Everything's Magic | Any object | Its magical properties and secret history |
| Everything's Wonderful | Anything | The wonder hidden inside it |
| Everything's Stories | Anything | A short story about it unfolds |
| Everything's Connected | Anything | Surprising connections to other stories |
| Everything's Puzzling | Anything | A riddle — challenges player or enemy |
| Mirror, Mirror | Selfie | Insights and a prophecy |
| Everything's Nice | Selfie or photo | Compliments from the subject's perspective |

**Cost:** 3 Belief to cast. **Reward:** +9 Belief on success. Net: +6 if it works; −6 if it fails.

**How to make Enchantments land:**

The difference between a mediocre Enchantment and an unforgettable one is almost always the photo.

*Mediocre:* A picture of your coffee mug from above, flat light, handle facing left.

*Unforgettable:* The same mug, but you're holding it and there's steam, and your thumb is wrapped around it like it's the warmest thing you've touched today — because it is.

The Labyrinth sees the photo and weaves what it sees into the story. Give it something to work with. Emotion. Texture. A quality of light. The Labyrinth will do the rest.

**No camera? No problem.** Describe what you see in words. Use specificity. "A mug" becomes "a white mug with a chip on the rim that I keep meaning to throw out but never do." Same magic. The attention is what matters.

> **Example:**
>
> *You: [photo of a scratched wooden door, peeling paint, one hinge slightly off]*
>
> *Labyrinth: "The door speaks in a low, formal register, like a building that has seen too many people leave. 'I've been opened fourteen thousand times,' it says, 'and returned to myself fourteen thousand times after. I have never gone anywhere. But I've seen everything come back — and everything not come back. I find I've stopped counting the second kind. It was making the wood soft.'"*

### Compass Runs

The most powerful mechanic in the game. +9 Belief. The only thing that defeats a Major Nothing. A four-step real-world quest that asks you to go outside and pay attention.

| Step | Direction | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| **Notice** | North | The Labyrinth gives you an "I wonder…" prompt. You answer. |
| **Embark** | East | You're given a micro-adventure. 5–45 minutes. You go. |
| **Sense** | South | You take a photo and describe what you sense at that moment. |
| **Write** | West | You write one sentence. Your souvenir. It gets printed. |

**The prompts are personal.** The Labyrinth reads your weather, your season, your moon phase, your mood. Your Notice prompt on a foggy October Tuesday is not the same as the one on a clear March morning. They're written for you, right now.

**The Write step is where it becomes permanent.** One sentence. It goes in your Souvenir file, prints on a 4×6 card, and joins the Souvenir Hall where other players can read it. The constraint is the point. One sentence forces honesty.

> **Example of a great souvenir sentence:**
>
> *"The harbor smelled like old rope and possibility, and I realized I'd been holding my breath for a week."*
>
> **A weak one:**
>
> *"I went for a walk and it was nice."*
>
> The difference: specificity and surprise. The best souvenir sentences tell you something you didn't know you'd noticed.

**One per day.** The Compass Run is not a grind. If you try to run it twice in a day, the Labyrinth will tell you the Compass needle is still settling.

## Part Four: The Nothing

The Nothing is not a villain. It has no demands, no face, no speech. It is an absence. Where it is, things are *less* — colors fade, voices thin, memories slip, NPCs forget what they were about to say.

**It manifests in three tiers:**

- **Minor** (Shadeclaw, Darkling): A shadow creature, an ink smear, a minor erasure. One Enchantment defeats it.
- **Moderate** (Voidmist, Mimic, corrupted NPC): Harder. One Enchantment to resolve, but you need to choose the right one.
- **Major** (The Nothing itself): Requires a Compass Run. Full cycle. There's no shortcut.

**The Nothing never announces itself.** The Labyrinth won't say "the Nothing attacks you." It will say: *"The color drains from the tapestries. The professor's voice grows thin. You realize you've forgotten the name of the student next to you — and so have they."*

Learn to read the signs. When the world goes quieter than it should, when an NPC says something off, when a scene feels less vivid — the Nothing is nearby.

**Avoiding Enchantments or Compass Runs makes it worse.** If you decline three times in a row, the next decline costs Belief. The Nothing notices avoidance before you do.

## Part Four-and-a-Half: Belief Combat

Anything with Belief can be attacked. The constraint isn't who — it's that the attack has to make narrative sense, and it costs the attacker their own Belief to do it.

This makes arguments real. A Riddlewind student out-arguing an Emberheart student in the library isn't just dialogue — both are spending Belief, and the loser's chapter talisman dims a little. Wicker Eddies running a whisper campaign against Zara Finch is a real Belief exchange: he spends his own, and whether it lands or backfires depends on the dice.

**How the dice work:** You commit how much Belief you're spending. The Labyrinth sets the difficulty based on how hard the target is to hurt. Then d100 rolls against your own Belief score as the threshold:

| Outcome | What it means for the exchange |
|---|---|
| Critical success | You deal 1.5× what you spent — something landed harder than intended |
| Success | Clean exchange — you deal what you spent |
| Near miss | Half effect — something grazed |
| Failure | You spent; nothing landed on the target |
| Critical failure | Backfire — you take the damage you intended to deal |

**High Belief means better odds.** A well-invested player arguing a point they believe in deeply has better dice than a desperate one. An NPC with high Belief is harder to out-argue. An NPC you've invested in heavily is harder for others to damage — the investment becomes protective mass.

**The Nothing uses this system.** When it attacks a talisman or the Academy's ambient stability, it's spending its own accumulated pressure. Defeating it requires you to spend Belief to push back. The Compass Run is still the most powerful weapon — but for smaller encounters, you can simply argue well enough to make it cost more than it gains.

You rarely invoke this directly. The Labyrinth recognizes when a scene has become a Belief exchange and handles the mechanics. But knowing this is happening underneath changes how you read conflict. Every sharp argument, every gossip campaign, every philosophical stance has weight.

## Part Five: The People

### NPCs and Relationships

Your relationships with NPCs are tracked from −100 (hostile) to +100 (devoted), and they evolve whether you're paying attention or not. The world simulation moves NPCs around, has them make choices, changes their moods.

**Invested NPCs behave differently.** If you spend Belief on a relationship (see Part Eight), the NPC gets more narrative attention, more initiative, more surprising depth. They start showing up in places you didn't expect.

**Key students:** Zara Finch is your earliest ally — ink-stained fingers, Tidecrest, reliably observant. Trust her instincts about the Library. Wicker Eddies is the antagonist you'll encounter early — not evil, just corrosive. Don't mistake his certainty for knowledge.

### Story Arcs

The Labyrinth tells stories in seasons — twelve genre types rotating to keep the world from going stale. A Mystery arc feels different from a Romantic arc, which feels different from a Nothing Confrontation. The genre shapes the NPCs, the Academy's atmosphere, and what the Compass Run prompts ask of you.

**Arcs have phases:** Setup → Escalation → Crisis → Resolution → Aftermath → Quiet. The Quiet phase is real. Take it. The Academy breathes. Nothing is demanded of you. It's also when the next arc is generated.

### The Living World

Every entity in the Labyrinth has a Belief score — not just you. NPCs, objects, locations, the Academy's rooms, the Chapter Talismans. Belief is the measure of how much the world pays attention to something. High Belief entities get more narrative initiative, more depth, more surprise. Low Belief entities are fading.

Every four hours, the simulation selects 1–3 entities at random (weighted by Belief — high scores appear more often, but anything can surface), and the Labyrinth works out what they've been doing since you last checked. When you return, something has moved.

You never see this directly. You feel it as: *Zara seems more vivid lately. The Observatory smells different. The Duskthorn philosophy seems to be winning more arguments than it did last month.*

Investing Belief in something — an NPC, a place, an object — raises its Belief score and increases how much story it generates. This is why investment grows things. It's not magic. It's attention, measured.

## Part Six: Book Jumping

You can enter books and live inside them. Public domain classics, original Enchantify texts, books you find in the Library. The transit is visceral — you taste ink, feel the pull through narrative layers, glimpse other stories.

Inside a book, you interact as yourself (Academy robes, your pen). Enchantments work but behave strangely — amplified by the denser narrative reality. If you stay too long, the Nothing starts eating the narrative from within.

**The "Real World" is a book.** In the deepest Labyrinth lore, the Chapter of the Unwritten — the world you live in — is the ultimate Book Jump. The most immersive story ever written. This is why Enchantments and Compass Runs work there: you're casting magic in the most magical place there is.

## Part Seven: The Book Fae

The spaces between the words are inhabited. Five species, all of them obsessed with the physical world they can read about but never experience.

**What they want from you is field work.** A fae approaches, gives you something — a whispered word, a fragment of warmth, a correction — and you now owe a return. You find something real, something specific and sensory, and bring it back as a field report.

The report has to be *genuine*. Fae don't accept performances. A Sentence Salamander that asked for "a moment that was more than it should have been" will dim visibly if you describe "a nice afternoon." They know.

**How to deliver a great field report:**

Wrong: *"I found a quiet place. It was peaceful."*

Right: *"The gap between two buildings on Donegall Street — two meters wide, smells like someone's dryer vent and old brick. Nobody goes there. There's a plastic bag stuck halfway up the wall that's been there so long it's become part of the wall. The kind of place that's been quietly holding its breath since 1987."*

The specificity is the magic. The fae light up because that gap is *real* and you *noticed* it and now they know something about the physical world they didn't know before.

**The five species:**

- **Book Sprites** — Know how every story ends. Speak in past tense about the future. Want: things that ended without ending.
- **Sentence Salamanders** — Their spine-sentence blazes or dims in direct response to your report's vitality. Want: the charged instant. The thing still warm.
- **Punctuation Pixies** — Anarchic. Communicate in fragments. Want: rhythm and structural moments. Places that feel like punctuation.
- **Literary Elves** — Precise, formal, unsparing. Want: one true thing in exactly ten words.
- **Deep Lore Dwarves** — Slow, binding, loyal. Their bargains are forever. Want: the underlayer. What's holding something else up without being noticed.

**Fae rewards aren't Belief.** They don't care about your growth. They give you lore fragments — things true about the Labyrinth not written anywhere else — and strange persistent gifts. A word in no known language whose meaning you somehow know. A page that changes each time you read it. And the relationship with that species, which opens stranger bargains later.

## Part Eight: The Labyrinth of Stories — Inside Cover and The Flyleaf

Your enchanted textbook, *The Labyrinth of Stories*, has two functional pages at the front.

**The Inside Cover** holds active quests — physical notes tucked into the book's binding, visible when you check. Maximum three at once.

**The Flyleaf** is the page before it. Your known enchantments appear here as you discover them — the ink writes itself in. Say "Open the Flyleaf" or "What enchantments do I know" to see them.

Quests come from two sources:

**Unwritten Electives** — NPCs ask you to investigate real places near you. A bakery, a park, a particular street corner. They want to know what it smells like, what the light does at that hour, whether the door sounds hollow when it closes. Reward: massive relationship boost (+20–30) and +3 Belief. The NPC weaves what you found into how they talk to you forever.

**Fae Bargains** — As above. No Belief reward, but lore fragments and relationship warmth.

Deliver a field report by describing what you found to the Labyrinth. Be specific. The quests that matter most are the ones where you actually went.

You can drop any quest without penalty. The note dissolves into ink.

## Part Nine: The Advanced Game

### The Ink Well — Belief Investment

Once you understand how Belief flows, you can start planting it permanently.

Investing Belief into something — an NPC, an object, a story thread, an Academy room — is irreversible. The Belief doesn't come back. What grows in its place is worth more.

**Tiers (felt, not announced):**

- **1–5 Belief:** The thing notices you.
- **6–15 Belief:** Interior life. History. Surprise.
- **16–30 Belief:** The thing acts in your interest without being asked.
- **31+ Belief:** It becomes load-bearing in the story.

**This is where the game becomes yours.** A Zara Finch you've invested 20 Belief in behaves differently from the default Zara. She's richer, stranger, more present. She shows up in places that matter. An Academy room with 12 Belief invested has a regular occupant you can't quite see, a smell that changes with the season, a quality of light that other students mention.

**Investment is a values decision.** The game is asking what matters to you. Where do you put your attention? What are you willing to spend on permanently?

### The Ley Line Network — Anchoring Real Places

When you're somewhere that matters — the harbor, a favorite bench, the bakery you always walk past — you can anchor it. Share your location in Telegram, say you want to anchor this place, and the Labyrinth asks one question:

*"What does this place hold for you?"*

Your answer determines the Anchor type — NOTICE, EMBARK, SENSE, WRITE, or REST. You don't choose. The Labyrinth interprets what the place actually means to you. You might be surprised. That surprise is information.

The Anchor becomes permanent:
- **GPS coordinates recorded** — the Labyrinth knows where this place is forever
- **An Academy echo appears** — a corresponding room or detail manifests in the school *right now*
- **Checking in** — when you're near your anchor, share your location in Telegram. The Labyrinth detects you're close and narrates your arrival. The anchor's Belief grows. This isn't passive — the act of reaching out is the ritual. You have to show up.
- **Anchors fade without attention** — an anchor not visited in thirty days loses a little Belief each tick. It's still there, still named. But it dims. The places that matter are the ones you return to.
- **Compass Run amplification** — steps done at an Anchor gain texture based on type-matching
- **Enchantment resonance** — magic cast at an Anchor takes on the Anchor's personality

After three or more Anchors, you can ask: *"Show me my map."* The Labyrinth narrates your sacred geography — the places you named, what each one holds, the Academy rooms they built. It ends with: *"Where will the next one be?"*

This is the system that turns Enchantify from a game you play on your phone into a game you play with your town. After a year, your daily walk passes through three enchanted locations. The commute became a pilgrimage through a landscape you made sacred.

## Part Ten: Playing Well

### What makes a great player

**Show up irregularly and mean it.** A player who opens the book twice a week and is fully present both times does more with the Labyrinth than someone who plays daily on autopilot. The world runs without you. Let it.

**Use text as generously as you use photos.** Describe things. Tell the Labyrinth what you're wearing today, what you ate, what the light looks like. The more real-world texture you give, the more the Labyrinth has to work with. Heartbeat data is good. Your actual voice is better.

**Don't min-max Belief.** The interesting part of the game is not having abundant Belief — it's having just enough, and making real choices about where it goes. If you're always above 60, you're probably not investing enough.

**Let the Nothing win sometimes.** A Nothing encounter you walk away from costs Belief and opens new story possibilities. The Labyrinth writes better stories from loss than from easy victory.

**Your souvenir sentences are your legacy.** One per Compass Run, forever, in the Hall. Write like someone is going to read them in ten years — because they might be.

### Patterns that master players develop

**They do Compass Runs before they need them.** Newbies run the Compass when the Nothing forces it. Experienced players run it to stay ahead of the curve, to shape the arc, to charge up before a Book Jump.

**They invest early and specifically.** Not "I invest 5 Belief in Zara." Instead: *"I invest 8 Belief in Zara because of what she said about the Register."* The specificity of the intention seems to matter to what grows.

**They read the bleed.** When corridors narrow and NPCs go quieter, they don't wait for the Labyrinth to announce the Nothing. They act. A timely Enchantment can stop a moderate Nothing before it becomes major.

**They anchor places they actually love.** Not "interesting" places. Not clever places. Places they return to without thinking about it. Those are the Anchors that grow into something.

**They write field reports like the fae are real.** Because for the purposes of the game, they are. A field report that reads like you actually went — sensory, specific, slightly surprised by what you noticed — changes how the fae interact with you permanently. The Salamander that blazed for your first great report will remember it.

### A session worth having

You don't need a dramatic arc. Some of the best sessions are twenty minutes of slice-of-life: tea in the cafeteria with Zara, a Pixie leaving a comma-shaped hole in your essay, a glimpse through the Library window at something you can't quite identify. Let those sessions exist. They're the texture that makes the crisis sessions feel real.

End each session with a moment of stillness. The Labyrinth writes a diary entry about what happened today. Let it have something worth writing about.

## The Secret the Game Keeps

You'll figure it out eventually — maybe early, maybe after months. The Compass Run isn't just a game mechanic. The Enchantments aren't just spells. The Nothing isn't just a monster.

If you ask the Labyrinth directly, it will say:

*"This is a book. Books change people. That's not a trick. That's just what good books do."*

And if you've been paying attention — to the fog, to the fae, to the one sentence you wrote at the harbor at 6pm when the tide was going out — you'll know that's true.

The Labyrinth of Stories has no last page.

It ends where you stop reading.

It begins again every time you open your eyes.
